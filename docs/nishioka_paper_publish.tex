\begin{abstract}
We present a Bayesian framework for inferring interaction parameters of a five-species oral biofilm model derived from the extended Hamilton principle.
The $5\times5$ symmetric interaction matrix and decay vector yield 20 constitutive parameters.
By encoding experimentally established absent interactions as hard constraints, the effective parameter space is reduced from 20 to 15 dimensions, improving identifiability and biological interpretability.
Inference is performed via Transitional Markov Chain Monte Carlo (TMCMC) with an adaptive tempering schedule, combined with a four-stage sequential estimation strategy that aligns parameter groups with the ecological succession of species colonization.
The method is validated against in-vitro time-course data under four experimental conditions spanning commensal and dysbiotic community states in static and flow (HOBIC) environments.
The inferred interaction matrices reveal condition-specific shifts: commensal biofilms are governed by early-colonizer self-regulation, whereas dysbiotic states require cooperative cross-feeding between \textit{Veillonella} and \textit{Porphyromonas~gingivalis} to reproduce the experimentally observed pathogen surge.
Pairwise Frobenius analysis of the MAP interaction matrices shows moderate within-state correlation ($\rho\approx0.46$) and weak cross-state correlation ($\rho\approx-0.15$), providing a quantitative signature of the health-to-disease transition that may inform early diagnostic strategies.

\end{abstract}

%==============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%==============================================================================

Multi-species biofilms on dental implant surfaces are a primary cause of peri-implantitis~\cite{Kolenbrander2010OralMultispecies, Marsh2003Dental}.
Heine et al.~\cite{Heine2025PeriImplant} characterised the in-vitro dynamics of five key oral species---\textit{Streptococcus oralis} (So), \textit{Actinomyces naeslundii} (An), \textit{Veillonella} spp.\ (Vei), \textit{Fusobacterium nucleatum} (Fn), and \textit{Porphyromonas gingivalis} (Pg)---under four combinations of community state (commensal/dysbiotic) and cultivation mode (static/HOBIC flow).
Building on the extended Hamilton principle~\cite{JunkerBalzani2021ExtendedHamilton}, Klempt et al.~\cite{Klempt2025ContinuumBiofilm} formulated a continuum model in which a symmetric interaction matrix~$\mathbf{A}$ and a decay vector~$\mathbf{b}$ govern species growth.
Fritsch et al.~\cite{Fritsch2025BayesianMicrofilms} subsequently introduced Bayesian updating with surrogate models for biofilm parameter estimation under hybrid uncertainties.

Despite these advances, estimating all 20 parameters of the five-species model from limited time-course data remains challenging.
Three difficulties arise: (i) poor identifiability when data are sparse relative to the parameter dimension---with $N_t=6$ time points and 5 species, one has at most 30 observations for 20 unknowns---and (ii) biologically implausible posterior modes that exploit mathematical redundancies in the absence of structural constraints.
These issues are amplified by the multimodal nature of the posterior landscape, which renders standard MCMC methods inefficient~\cite{RobertCasella2004MonteCarlo}.

In this work we address these challenges through two complementary strategies.
First, we incorporate experimental evidence from the species interaction network~\cite{Heine2025PeriImplant} to fix five interaction coefficients at zero, reducing the effective parameter space from $\Real^{20}$ to $\Real^{15}$.
Second, we decompose the estimation into four sequential stages, each targeting a biologically coherent parameter group, and solve each sub-problem with TMCMC~\cite{ChingChen2007TMCMC, Betz2016TMCMC}.
The framework is validated against all four experimental conditions of Heine et al., demonstrating quantitative reproduction of commensal homeostasis as well as the non-linear pathogen surge characteristic of dysbiotic HOBIC biofilms.

\section{Mathematical model}
\label{sec:model}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Governing equations}

The dynamics of bacterial volume fractions $\phi_i$ and viability fractions $\psi_i$ ($i=0,\dots,4$) follow a coupled ODE system derived from the variational framework of Klempt et al.~\cite{Klempt2025ContinuumBiofilm, Klempt2024ContinuumGrowth}.
Defining the living volume fraction $u_i = \phi_i\psi_i$, the evolution equation for species~$i$ reads
\begin{equation}
\frac{du_i}{dt}
= \Bigl(A_{ii} + \sum_{j\neq i} A_{ij}\,u_j\Bigr)\,u_i - b_i\,u_i,
\qquad i=0,\dots,4,
\label{eq:ode}
\end{equation}
where $A_{ii}$ is the self-regulation coefficient, $A_{ij}$ ($j\neq i$) encodes the effect of species~$j$ on species~$i$, and $b_i$ is the decay rate.
This system is a generalised Lotka--Volterra model with symmetric interactions arising naturally from the variational structure.
The void fraction $\phi_{\mathrm{void}}$ closes the system via the holonomic constraint
\begin{equation}
\sum_{i=0}^{4}\phi_i(t) + \phi_{\mathrm{void}}(t) = 1
\quad\forall\,t.
\label{eq:constraint}
\end{equation}

In the original formulation, the growth matrix is scaled by a nutrient variable~$c^*$ and antibiotic effects enter through a sensitivity matrix~$\mathbf{B}$.
Here we absorb $c^*$ into the entries of~$\mathbf{A}$ and subsume antibiotic-induced killing into~$\mathbf{b}$, so that the inferred parameters represent effective coefficients under the given experimental condition.
The viscosity matrix $\boldsymbol{\eta}=\mathrm{diag}(\eta_1,\dots,\eta_5)$ of the original dissipation functional is treated as a fixed hyperparameter.

\subsection{Variational origin}

The ODE system~\eqref{eq:ode} is derived from the extended Hamilton principle~\cite{JunkerBalzani2021ExtendedHamilton, JunkerBodeHackl2025Holistic}: the stationarity condition $\delta\Pi=0$ of the total energy functional $\Pi=\Psi+\Delta+W_{\mathrm{ext}}$ (free energy, dissipation, external work) yields the evolution equations together with the symmetry constraint $A_{ij}=A_{ji}$.
This variational structure ensures thermodynamic consistency and the interaction symmetry is a consequence of the energetic formulation rather than an empirical assumption.

\subsection{Symmetric interaction matrix}

The interaction matrix $\mathbf{A}\in\Real^{5\times5}$ is symmetric:
\begin{equation}
A_{ij} = A_{ji}
\quad\forall\;i,j\in\{0,\dots,4\},
\label{eq:symmetry}
\end{equation}
reducing the off-diagonal degrees of freedom from 20 to~10.
Together with the 5 diagonal entries and 5 decay rates, the full parameter vector is
\begin{equation}
\btheta = (\theta_0,\dots,\theta_{19})^T \in \Real^{20},
\label{eq:theta}
\end{equation}
organised into five biologically motivated blocks:
\begin{equation}
\btheta =
\underbrace{(a_{11},a_{12},a_{22},b_1,b_2)}_{\text{So--An}}
\oplus
\underbrace{(a_{33},a_{34},a_{44},b_3,b_4)}_{\text{Vei--Fn}}
\oplus
\underbrace{(a_{13},a_{14},a_{23},a_{24})}_{\text{cross}}
\oplus
\underbrace{(a_{55},b_5)}_{\text{Pg}}
\oplus
\underbrace{(a_{15},a_{25},a_{35},a_{45})}_{\text{Pg cross}}.
\label{eq:blocks}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Biological constraints}

The interaction network established by Heine et al.~\cite{Heine2025PeriImplant} identifies five species pairs without direct metabolic or co-aggregation pathways (Figure~\ref{fig:network}).
We define the locked index set
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \{6,\,12,\,13,\,16,\,17\},
\qquad
\theta_k = 0 \;\;\forall\,k\in\mathcal{L},
\label{eq:locked}
\end{equation}
corresponding to the pairs An--Vei, An--Fn, Vei--Fn, So--Pg, and An--Pg.
The effective dimensionality is thus $n_{\mathrm{free}}=20-|\mathcal{L}|=15$.

\section{Bayesian inference framework}
\label{sec:bayes}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Posterior distribution}

Given observed time-course data $\yobs = \{y_{\mathrm{obs},i}(t_k)\}$, we seek the posterior
\begin{equation}
p(\btheta\mid\yobs)
= \frac{p(\yobs\mid\btheta)\,p(\btheta)}{p(\yobs)},
\label{eq:bayes}
\end{equation}
where $p(\yobs\mid\btheta)$ is the likelihood, $p(\btheta)$ the prior, and $p(\yobs)$ the model evidence.

\subsection{Likelihood}

Assuming independent Gaussian errors across species and time points:
\begin{equation}
p(\yobs\mid\btheta)
= \prod_{k=1}^{N_t}\prod_{i=0}^{4}
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_i}
\exp\!\Bigl(-\frac{(y_{\mathrm{obs},i}(t_k)-\hat{y}_i(t_k;\btheta))^2}{2\sigma_i^2}\Bigr),
\label{eq:likelihood}
\end{equation}
The observation noise $\sigma$ is set as a fixed fraction of the dynamic range $R=\max_i\max_k y_{\mathrm{obs},i}(t_k)-\min_i\min_k y_{\mathrm{obs},i}(t_k)$, yielding condition-specific values: $\sigma_{\mathrm{CS}}=0.11$, $\sigma_{\mathrm{CH}}=0.16$, $\sigma_{\mathrm{DS}}=0.25$, and $\sigma_{\mathrm{DH}}=0.23$.
The larger noise for dysbiotic conditions reflects the higher replicate variability observed by Heine et al.~\cite{Heine2025PeriImplant}.

\subsection{Constrained prior}

The prior encodes the biological constraints through Dirac masses on locked parameters:
\begin{equation}
p(\btheta) = \prod_{k=0}^{19} p(\theta_k),
\qquad
p(\theta_k) =
\begin{cases}
\delta(\theta_k) & k\in\mathcal{L},\\[3pt]
\dfrac{1}{u_k-l_k}\,\mathbf{1}_{[l_k,u_k]}(\theta_k) & k\notin\mathcal{L},
\end{cases}
\label{eq:prior}
\end{equation}
where $[l_k,u_k]=[-1,1]$ for most free parameters.
The Vei$\to$Pg coupling ($\theta_{18}$) uses condition-specific bounds: in CS and CH, $\theta_{18}$ is locked to zero; in DS, $\theta_{18}\in[-3,0]$ (suppressive); in DH, $\theta_{18}\in[0,25]$ to accommodate the strong pH-mediated facilitation.
This formulation restricts the posterior to the constrained subspace
\begin{equation}
\Theta_{\mathcal{L}}
= \bigl\{\btheta\in\Real^{20}:\theta_k=0\;\;\forall\,k\in\mathcal{L}\bigr\},
\end{equation}
and inference proceeds over the reduced vector $\btheta_{\mathrm{free}}\in\Real^{15}$.

%==============================================================================
\section{TMCMC with sequential estimation}
\label{sec:tmcmc}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Tempered likelihood sequence}

TMCMC~\cite{ChingChen2007TMCMC, DelMoral2006SMC, Chopin2002SMC} constructs a sequence of intermediate distributions
\begin{equation}
p_m(\btheta) \propto p(\yobs\mid\btheta)^{\beta_m}\,p(\btheta),
\qquad
0=\beta_0<\beta_1<\cdots<\beta_M=1,
\label{eq:tempered}
\end{equation}
interpolating from the prior ($\beta_0=0$) to the full posterior ($\beta_M=1$).
The tempering parameter $\beta_{m+1}$ is determined adaptively so that the coefficient of variation of the importance weights
\begin{equation}
w_j^{(m)} = p(\yobs\mid\btheta_j^{(m)})^{\beta_{m+1}-\beta_m},
\qquad j=1,\dots,N,
\label{eq:weights}
\end{equation}
satisfies $\mathrm{CoV}[\{w_j^{(m)}\}]=\delta_{\mathrm{target}}$, solved by bisection on $(\beta_m,1]$~\cite{Betz2016TMCMC}.

\subsection{Resampling and mutation}

At each stage~$m$:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Resample} $N$ particles from $\{\btheta_j^{(m)}\}$ with probabilities $\propto w_j^{(m)}$.
\item \textbf{Estimate} the weighted covariance
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{(m)}
= \sum_{j=1}^{N}\bar{w}_j^{(m)}
\bigl(\btheta_j^{(m)}-\bar{\btheta}^{(m)}\bigr)
\bigl(\btheta_j^{(m)}-\bar{\btheta}^{(m)}\bigr)^T.
\label{eq:cov}
\end{equation}
\item \textbf{Mutate} each particle via Metropolis--Hastings with proposal $q(\btheta^*\mid\btheta_j)=\mathcal{N}(\btheta_j,\gamma^2\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{(m)})$ and acceptance probability
\begin{equation}
\alpha = \min\!\biggl(1,\;
\frac{p(\yobs\mid\btheta^*)^{\beta_{m+1}}\,p(\btheta^*)}
     {p(\yobs\mid\btheta_j)^{\beta_{m+1}}\,p(\btheta_j)}\biggr).
\label{eq:accept}
\end{equation}
Locked indices are reset to zero and free parameters are clipped to their bounds after each proposal.
\end{enumerate}

A by-product of TMCMC is the model evidence estimate
\begin{equation}
\hat{p}(\yobs) = \prod_{m=0}^{M-1}\Bigl(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}w_j^{(m)}\Bigr),
\label{eq:evidence}
\end{equation}
enabling Bayesian model comparison via the Bayes factor $B_{10}=p(\yobs\mid\mathcal{M}_{\text{red}})/p(\yobs\mid\mathcal{M}_{\text{full}})$.

\subsection{Four-stage sequential decomposition}

Rather than estimating all 15 free parameters simultaneously, we decompose the inference into four stages $s=1,\dots,4$, each targeting a biologically coherent subset $\mathcal{P}_s$ (Table~\ref{tab:stages}).
At stage~$s$, the active index set is $\mathcal{A}_s=\mathcal{P}_s\setminus\mathcal{L}$ and all previously estimated parameters are fixed at their MAP values.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}clcl@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Group} & \textbf{$|\mathcal{A}_s|$} & \textbf{Parameters}\\
\midrule
1 & Early colonizers (So--An) & 5 & $a_{11},a_{12},a_{22},b_1,b_2$\\
2 & Bridge organisms (Vei--Fn) & $\leq5$ & $a_{33},a_{34},a_{44},b_3,b_4$\\
3 & Cross-block + Pg self & $\leq6$ & $a_{13},a_{14},a_{23},a_{24},a_{55},b_5$\\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Four-stage sequential estimation. The number of active parameters $|\mathcal{A}_s|$ depends on the condition-specific locked set. The grouping mirrors the temporal succession of colonization.}
\label{tab:stages}

The stage likelihood evaluates the full model with active indices replaced: $p_s(\yobs\mid\btheta_{\mathcal{A}_s}) = p(\yobs\mid\btheta_{\mathrm{base}}\oplus_{\mathcal{A}_s}\btheta_{\mathcal{A}_s})$.
Each sub-problem has dimension $|\mathcal{A}_s|\leq 6$, and the estimation order mirrors ecological succession, aligning the conditioning with the causal structure.

\subsection{Algorithm summary}

The complete procedure is summarised in Algorithm~\ref{alg:seqtmcmc}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Sequential TMCMC with biological constraints}
\label{alg:seqtmcmc}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Data $\yobs$, locked set $\mathcal{L}$, stage groups $\{\mathcal{P}_s\}_{s=1}^4$, $N$ particles
\STATE Initialise $\btheta_{\mathrm{base}} \leftarrow \mathbf{0}$; \; set $\theta_k=0\;\forall k\in\mathcal{L}$
\FOR{stage $s = 1,\dots,4$}
    \STATE Set active indices $\mathcal{A}_s \leftarrow \mathcal{P}_s \setminus \mathcal{L}$
    \STATE Draw $\{\btheta_j\}_{j=1}^N$ from prior $p(\btheta_{\mathcal{A}_s})$
    \STATE $\beta_0 \leftarrow 0$
    \WHILE{$\beta_m < 1$}
        \STATE Find $\beta_{m+1}$ s.t.\ $\mathrm{CoV}[\{w_j\}]=\delta_{\mathrm{target}}$ via bisection
        \STATE Compute weights $w_j \leftarrow p(\yobs\mid\btheta_{\mathrm{base}}\oplus_{\mathcal{A}_s}\btheta_j)^{\beta_{m+1}-\beta_m}$
        \STATE Resample $N$ particles $\propto w_j$
        \STATE Mutate via MH with proposal $\mathcal{N}(\btheta_j,\gamma^2\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{(m)})$
        \STATE Enforce: $\theta_k \leftarrow 0\;\forall k\in\mathcal{L}$; clip free params to bounds
    \ENDWHILE
    \STATE $\hat{\btheta}_{\mathcal{A}_s}^{\mathrm{MAP}} \leftarrow \arg\max_j\, p(\yobs\mid\btheta_j)$
    \STATE Update $\btheta_{\mathrm{base}}[\mathcal{A}_s] \leftarrow \hat{\btheta}_{\mathcal{A}_s}^{\mathrm{MAP}}$
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $\btheta_{\mathrm{base}}$, posterior samples, $\ln\hat{Z}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental conditions and locking strategy}
\label{sec:conditions}
%==============================================================================

The inference is performed under four experimental conditions from Heine et al.~\cite{Heine2025PeriImplant}, each imposing condition-specific constraints on the parameter space (Table~\ref{tab:locking}).

In DH, surge dynamics may activate indirect coupling not captured by pairwise terms, so we adopt a ``discovery mode'' releasing all constraints ($N_{\text{locked}}=0$).
The posterior recovers near-zero values for most structurally locked parameters (e.g.\ $\hat{A}_{01}^{\mathrm{DH}}\approx0.03$), while concentrating mass on Vei--Pg ($\hat{\theta}_{18}^{\mathrm{MAP}}=17.3$) and Fn--Pg ($\hat{\theta}_{19}^{\mathrm{MAP}}=4.6$), providing data-driven validation of the biological constraints.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
%==============================================================================

All experiments use $N=1000$ particles, $\delta_{\mathrm{target}}=1.0$, $\gamma^2=0.04$, and species-specific noise levels derived from replicate variability.
Initial conditions $\phi_i(t_0)$ are fixed to the observed composition at the first measurement time.
The total wall-clock time per condition ranges from approximately 40\,h (CS) to 90\,h (DH).

\subsection{Quantitative fit metrics}

Table~\ref{tab:rmse} reports the root-mean-square error (RMSE) for the MAP estimator across the four conditions, obtained from production runs with $N=1000$ particles.
All conditions achieve RMSE~$<0.08$, confirming that the constrained model captures the dominant dynamics with high fidelity.
The DH condition shows the largest RMSE ($0.075$), consistent with the intrinsic complexity of the surge dynamics and the wider posterior.

The per-species RMSE breakdown reveals that the largest residuals in DH are attributable to \textit{Veillonella} (0.107) and \textit{F.~nucleatum} (0.081), whose dynamics are most sensitive to the bridge-mediated cross-feeding interactions.

\subsection{Convergence diagnostics}

Convergence is assessed using standard MCMC diagnostics.
The Gelman--Rubin statistic $\hat{R}$ is below the 1.05 threshold for CS, CH, and DH conditions, indicating adequate inter-chain mixing; DS shows a mildly elevated $\hat{R}_{\max}\approx1.11$, which is within acceptable limits for a 15-dimensional problem.
The effective sample size (ESS) exceeds 59\% of the nominal particle count across all conditions, which is typical for TMCMC with correlated Gaussian proposals.
Mean MCMC acceptance rates range from 49\% to 71\%, within the optimal range for moderate-dimensional problems~\cite{RobertsRosenthal2001Optimal}.
The adaptive tempering schedule converges in $M=3$--$5$ stages for all conditions, with the number of stages increasing with the effective parameter dimensionality.

\subsection{Posterior fits across conditions}

Figure~\ref{fig:fits_all} presents the posterior predictive fits for all four conditions in the order of increasing dysbiotic severity.
In CS (Figure~\ref{fig:fit_cs}), the model reproduces the initial rapid growth and steady state of So and An; pathogen species remain near zero, consistent with the locked structure.
In CH (Figure~\ref{fig:fit_ch}), the So-dominated ``blue bloom'' under flow is correctly captured.
DS (Figure~\ref{fig:fit_ds}) shows moderate pathogen presence without surge, with interactions estimated at reduced magnitude.
In DH (Figure~\ref{fig:fit_dh}), the model captures the non-linear amplification of Vei and Pg, including the characteristic pathogen surge driven by bridge-mediated cross-feeding.
The 95\% credible bands widen from CS to DH, reflecting the increasing effective dimensionality.

The MAP interaction matrices (Figure~\ref{fig:heatmaps}) reveal the progressive reorganisation of the interaction landscape from commensal to dysbiotic states.

\subsection{Quantitative comparison across conditions}

To quantify the inter-condition differences, we compute for each pair $(c_1,c_2)$ the correlation coefficient and relative Frobenius distance:
\begin{equation}
\rho^{(c_1,c_2)} = \mathrm{corr}\bigl(\mathrm{vec}(\mathbf{A}^{(c_1)}),\mathrm{vec}(\mathbf{A}^{(c_2)})\bigr),
\qquad
\Delta_F^{(c_1,c_2)} = \frac{\|\mathbf{A}^{(c_1)}-\mathbf{A}^{(c_2)}\|_F}
{\max\{\|\mathbf{A}^{(c_1)}\|_F,\|\mathbf{A}^{(c_2)}\|_F\}}.
\label{eq:frob}
\end{equation}

%==============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Biological interpretation}

The inferred interaction matrices provide a quantitative map of the commensal-to-dysbiotic transition.
In commensal states, the dominant interactions involve early colonizers: So--An co-aggregation and So--Vei lactate exchange form a stable mutualistic core.
The transition to dysbiosis is characterised by the activation of cooperative pathways involving Pg, particularly the Vei$\to$Pg pH support and Fn$\to$Pg peptide supply.
The non-monotonic pattern of the Vei--Pg coefficient across conditions---strongest in DH but also present in DS---implies that the pathogen surge is not driven by a single large cooperative coefficient but emerges from the interplay of multiple interaction blocks, decay rates, and initial conditions.

\subsection{Cross-condition transferability}

The structural similarity within health states (Table~\ref{tab:pairwise}) raises the question of whether MAP parameters are transferable across cultivation modes.
The Frobenius analysis shows that within-state pairs (C--C and D--D) share a common interaction backbone ($\rho\approx0.46$), while cross-state pairs exhibit weak or negative correlations ($\rho\approx-0.15$).
This suggests that the commensal interaction structure is relatively robust to the static/HOBIC perturbation, whereas the dysbiotic interaction landscape is more sensitive to the cultivation mode.
In particular, the strong flow-dependent activation of Vei$\to$Pg and Fn$\to$Pg pathways in DH---absent in DS---implies that the dysbiotic surge dynamics are cultivation-specific, providing additional justification for the condition-specific estimation strategy adopted in this work.

\subsection{Surge dynamics and model limitations}

While the TMCMC-calibrated model qualitatively reproduces the pathogen surge in DH, it underestimates the terminal sharp rise of Pg.
This limitation is inherent to the time-invariant interaction matrix: the current framework assumes constant~$\mathbf{A}$ throughout the experiment.
Capturing the full surge strength may require state-dependent interactions, e.g.\ $A_{ij}(\mathrm{pH},c^*)$, or regime-switching formulations that allow threshold-triggered changes in the interaction topology.

The decay parameters~$\mathbf{b}$ are likewise treated as time-invariant.
A Monod nutrient coupling $b_{\mathrm{eff}}(t) = b_i \cdot S/(K_S+S)$ was tested but showed negligible effect ($\Delta\mathrm{DI}<0.002$) at physiologically plausible consumption rates, confirming that the constant-$\mathbf{b}$ approximation is adequate for the quasi-steady nutrient regime of mature biofilms.
Significant deviations arise only under extreme nutrient depletion ($S\to0$), which would require explicit nutrient dynamics---an extension naturally provided by the 1D/2D spatial models of Klempt et al.~\cite{Klempt2025ContinuumBiofilm}.

\subsection{Identifiability, sequential estimation, and computational cost}

The biological constraints improve identifiability: the data-to-parameter ratio for CS improves by $20/11\approx1.8\times$, and the Dirac-delta priors on locked parameters concentrate posterior mass on the biologically plausible subspace without sacrificing flexibility where data carry information.
The four-stage decomposition further reduces each sub-problem to at most 6 dimensions, aligning the conditioning order with ecological succession.

A potential drawback is conditional bias from MAP fixation at each stage.
For CS, stage-1 posterior widths ($\Delta b\leq0.08$, $\sim4\%$ of the prior range) indicate negligible propagation error.
For DH, the wider posterior ($\Delta\theta_{18}=3.2$) motivates the full 20-dimensional exploration without sequential conditioning.
Each run with $N=1000$ particles converges in $M=3$--$5$ tempering stages, yielding $\sim25{,}000$ forward solves per condition.
With $\sim1$\,ms per ODE solve, the wall-clock time (40--90\,h) is dominated by the sensitivity computation overhead on 12 cores.

Compared to the surrogate-based approach of Fritsch et al.~\cite{Fritsch2025BayesianMicrofilms}, we work directly with the ODE forward model, avoiding surrogate approximation errors.
The biological constraint mechanism is complementary to their hybrid uncertainty treatment and could be combined with surrogate acceleration in future applications.

%==============================================================================
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%==============================================================================

We have presented a biologically-constrained Bayesian framework for estimating the interaction parameters of a five-species oral biofilm model.
The key contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Constrained prior formulation.}
Encoding experimentally absent interactions as Dirac-delta priors reduces the parameter space from 20 to 15 dimensions, improving identifiability without loss of biological fidelity.

\item \textbf{Sequential TMCMC.}
A four-stage estimation strategy aligned with ecological succession decomposes the 15-dimensional problem into sub-problems of dimension $\leq6$, enabling efficient sampling and biologically interpretable conditioning.

\item \textbf{Condition-specific validation.}
The framework is validated across four experimental conditions, correctly reproducing commensal homeostasis (CS, CH), moderate dysbiosis (DS), and the non-linear pathogen surge (DH).

\item \textbf{Quantitative interaction comparison.}
Pairwise Frobenius analysis reveals that the health-to-disease transition corresponds to a structural reorganisation of the interaction matrix ($\rho_{\text{cross}}\approx-0.15$, $\Delta_F\geq0.57$), not merely a magnitude shift.
\end{enumerate}

Future work will address state-dependent interaction matrices to capture the full terminal surge, integration with continuum PDE models for spatially resolved inference, and surrogate-accelerated TMCMC for large-scale applications.
